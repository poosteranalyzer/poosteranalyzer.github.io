Insights
How insights are calculated
Each badge applies a fixed rule to the numbers extracted from your PDF:

• Whitespace — pixels not covered by text or images, as % of total area
• Word count — exact count from the PDF text layer
• Palette — colors with >1% coverage after k-means clustering
• Contrast — WCAG formula: ratio of luminance between avg text color and avg background
• Legibility — median font size from the PDF, flagged against the ≥24pt rule for A0 posters at 1.5 m
Thresholds are heuristics from design guidelines, not empirical poster research.

All green? That's a good sign — but it doesn't mean the poster is finished. These checks flag obvious problems; they can't judge clarity of message, logical flow, figure quality, or whether your story lands. They also can't tell you where a viewer's eye will actually go first — whether your layout follows a natural Z or F reading path, or whether your title, key figure, and conclusion form a clear visual sequence. Use this as a starting point, not a sign-off.

Palette
How the palette is extracted
The rendered poster is sampled (~7,000 pixels) and grouped into 8 color clusters using k-means++ — an algorithm that repeatedly asks "which pixels are most similar?" until it finds 8 representative colors.

The bar width shows how much of the poster each color covers.
Near-white pixels are excluded so background doesn't dominate. Results vary slightly each run due to random initialization. Colours inside photos and figures are included, which may inflate the count if your poster contains images with a wide colour range.
Space Breakdown
How space is classified

• Text — exact bounding boxes from the PDF text layer. No pixel guessing.

• Whitespace — smooth, uniform pixels whose colour matches the poster's background (sampled from the page corners). True breathing room: margins, gutters, and empty areas. Solid-coloured boxes in a different colour are not counted as whitespace.

• Graphics — everything else: photos, charts, illustrations, and also solid-coloured design fills (section backgrounds, coloured boxes, borders). Anything that is not whitespace or text ends up here.
Thin decorative lines and subtle textures sit on the boundary — they may be split between whitespace and images depending on their contrast.

Words
Legibility & Accessibility
Legibility — font sizes come directly from the PDF's transform matrix (exact, not estimated). The median font size is used as the "body text" representative. The ≥24pt rule is based on the standard recommendation that body text on an A0 poster should be readable at 1.5 metres.

Contrast ratio — how different the text colour is from the background, using the WCAG 2.1 formula. The scale runs from 1:1 (invisible — same colour) to 21:1 (maximum — pure black on pure white).

Common reference points:
• 21:1 — black on white (maximum)
• 7:1 — dark gray on white (WCAG AAA)
• 4.5:1 — WCAG AA (web body text minimum)
• 3:1 — WCAG AA Large (minimum for 18 pt+ text)
• 2:1 — light gray on white (hard to read)
• 1:1 — invisible

Poster text is always large (≥18 pt), so 3:1 is the practical minimum — though aiming for 4.5:1+ is better for accessibility.
This tool samples the most ink-like pixels in text regions and compares against the background. Individual text/background pairs may vary from this overall estimate.
Text Hierarchy
How hierarchy is measured
Every text item in the PDF has an exact font size stored in its transform matrix. We collect all these sizes and find three representative levels:

• Title — the largest font size found
• Heading — the 15th percentile (upper range, excluding the outlier title)
• Body — the 50th percentile (median, representing typical body text)

Bars show each level as a % of the title size, so a flat chart means poor hierarchy.

The type scale below lists every distinct font size found, its role, its point size, and what percentage of text items use it.
Decorative elements (logos, captions) can skew results if they use unusual sizes.
Spatial Maps
How spatial maps are built
Each pixel of the rendered poster is assigned to exactly one category:

• Text (green) — inside a PDF text bounding box
• Empty (blue) — near-white rendered pixel, outside text boxes
• Graphics (orange) — everything else: photos, charts, and coloured design fills

The three maps highlight each category in color against a gray background, so you can see where each element type lives spatially on the poster.
